# Integrable Systems Guide: Research-Radar Reorganization Plan

**Status:** discussion draft; records decisions and open questions before implementation  
**Last updated:** 2026-07-19

## 1. Why this document exists

The current site mixes several different activities:

1. reporting newly released research;
2. recording papers discovered by the automated curation process;
3. constructing reading chains with older background papers;
4. representing the editor's own research interests and judgments.

These activities have different standards of freshness and authority. When they are displayed in one stream, an older paper added today can look like a new result, and an AI-generated reading suggestion can look like a human-endorsed recommendation. This weakens the site's value as a public research radar.

The purpose of the reorganization is therefore not to make the site larger. It is to make the meaning of each displayed item clear.

## 2. Target positioning

The public site should be:

> A selective research radar for new developments in integrable structures and the mathematical or physical consequences produced by those structures.

It should not be:

- a mirror of the latest arXiv listings;
- a comprehensive map of the whole integrable-systems field;
- a daily quota of papers;
- a public version of all personal reading notes;
- a place where automatically generated background chains are presented as fully reviewed scholarship.

The main editorial questions for every public paper are:

1. **What new integrable structure is introduced, revealed, extended, or used?**
2. **What result becomes possible because of that structure?**

If neither question can be answered clearly, the paper should normally not appear in the public frontier stream.

## 3. Decisions already reached

### 3.1 Freshness is determined by the paper, not by the site

Public ordering should use the actual research event date:

- first public release;
- substantial revision;
- first formal online journal publication.

The date on which the site discovered or featured a paper is editorial history only. It should not move an old paper to the top of the homepage.

### 3.2 Do not use old papers to fill a daily quota

There is no requirement to publish three to seven papers every day. A day may contain zero, one, or several qualifying papers.

Recent searches should be broader than the original DNLS/Fokas--Lenells-centered workflow. With a broader but still selective scope, recent papers should normally provide enough material without adding old background papers to the frontier stream.

### 3.3 The site must add editorial value beyond arXiv

A paper is not selected merely because it is new or classified under `nlin.SI`.

The site's added value should be:

- structural filtering;
- novelty filtering;
- concise explanation of the main result;
- explanation of the connection to core integrable ideas;
- exclusion of routine or repetitive applications.

### 3.4 Scope should be broader, but still explainable

The site should not be limited to the editor's personal equations and collaborators. However, it should not publish papers whose role and innovation cannot be explained reliably to readers familiar with integrable PDEs, spectral methods, and nonlinear waves.

A technically unfamiliar paper may be included when the bridge to core integrable structures can be stated clearly. A paper whose main question and innovation remain opaque should stay in an internal candidate list rather than being published.

### 3.5 Automatic content and human-reviewed content must be distinguishable

Public content should eventually carry a review state such as:

- `automated`: metadata and abstract-grounded summary generated by the curation workflow;
- `human-reviewed`: checked and accepted by the editor;
- optionally `pending-review`: retained internally but not promoted as authoritative content.

Automatically generated explanations should not be presented as personal endorsement.

## 4. Topic scope

The organizing principle is **integrable structure plus innovation**, not a fixed list of favorite equations.

### 4.1 Core structural frontier

These subjects form the main scope of the radar.

#### Spectral and Lax structures

- new or substantially extended Lax pairs and zero-curvature formulations;
- direct and inverse scattering in new settings;
- new spectral symmetries, singularities, branch structures, or reconstruction mechanisms;
- monodromy, scattering data, and spectral invariants with new consequences.

A formal Lax pair alone is not enough. Priority goes to work where the spectral structure enables a theorem, classification, reconstruction, control mechanism, or new analysis.

#### Integrable hierarchies and Hamiltonian structures

- new hierarchies, positive or negative flows, and reductions;
- recursion operators;
- bi-Hamiltonian or multi-Hamiltonian structures;
- Poisson pencils and systematic conservation-law generation;
- matrix, noncommutative, or multicomponent extensions with genuine structural content.

#### Riemann--Hilbert methods and asymptotics

- new Riemann--Hilbert formulations;
- nonlinear steepest descent and \(\bar\partial\) methods;
- soliton resolution;
- transition regions and critical asymptotics;
- nonzero backgrounds, boundaries, spectral singularities, low regularity, or multicomponent problems;
- new local models, often connected to Painlevé equations.

#### Tau functions, Darboux/Bäcklund structures, and exact solutions

- non-recursive, binary, vectorial, or matrix Darboux structures;
- systematic handling of high-order spectral degeneracy;
- KP/Toda reductions, Gram/Wronskian determinants, and tau-function structure;
- special-polynomial control of solution geometry;
- classification, decomposition, or asymptotic results for exact solutions.

Routine application of a standard Darboux transformation to another similar equation, followed mainly by plots of low-order solutions, should normally be excluded.

#### Finite-gap and algebraic-geometric structures

- new spectral curves, divisors, or Baker--Akhiezer constructions;
- finite-gap degeneration and soliton or rogue-wave limits;
- high-genus or continuum limits;
- links to modulation, stability, soliton gases, or Riemann--Hilbert methods;
- new realizability or reduction conditions.

#### Painlevé and isomonodromic structures

- new isomonodromic formulations;
- discrete Painlevé systems;
- Painlevé functions as critical asymptotic models;
- rational solutions and special-polynomial structures;
- direct links to random matrices, orthogonal polynomials, KPZ-type limits, or nonlinear waves.

#### Discrete integrability and integrable geometry

- integrable lattice equations and integrable discretizations;
- multidimensional consistency;
- Yang--Baxter maps;
- discrete Lax representations;
- discrete differential or conformal geometry;
- meaningful continuous limits and structure-preserving numerical schemes.

### 4.2 Structure-driven adjacent frontier

These subjects are eligible when their connection to integrable structure is explicit and explainable.

#### Quantum integrability

- Yang--Baxter equations and \(R\)-matrices;
- Bethe Ansatz, transfer matrices, quantum groups, and Yangians;
- quantum spectral curves;
- thermodynamic Bethe Ansatz;
- integrable circuits, quenches, and nonequilibrium dynamics.

The annotation should explain how the relevant spectral, scattering, or conservation structure relates to or differs from classical integrable systems.

#### Generalized hydrodynamics and integrable statistical physics

- generalized hydrodynamics;
- soliton-gas kinetic theory;
- thermodynamic and hydrodynamic limits;
- nonequilibrium transport;
- integrable turbulence and wave kinetics;
- experimentally testable macroscopic consequences of microscopic integrability.

#### Integrable probability and random matrices

- determinantal or Pfaffian structures;
- Fredholm determinants and integrable kernels;
- orthogonal-polynomial Riemann--Hilbert problems;
- exact distributions, Painlevé representations, and solvable particle systems.

The paper should use integrable structure as an essential method, not merely mention an integrable model in the background.

#### Experiments, control, inverse problems, and data-driven integrability

- measurement or control of scattering data;
- nonlinear Fourier methods;
- soliton tomography and spectral sensing;
- inverse design using integrable variables;
- experimentally verified consequences of integrable theory;
- discovery of Lax pairs, conservation laws, or hidden integrability from data.

The integrable structure must participate directly in prediction, control, reconstruction, or experimental interpretation.

### 4.3 Personal high-priority directions

The following remain important monitoring priorities, but they no longer define the entire public scope:

- DNLS and derivative-NLS-type systems;
- Kaup--Newell, Chen--Lee--Liu, Gerdjikov--Ivanov, and Fokas--Lenells systems;
- coupled and multicomponent NLS-type equations;
- IST, Riemann--Hilbert methods, and long-time asymptotics;
- finite-gap solutions and soliton gases;
- Darboux transformations, rogue waves, and breathers;
- work related to Liming Ling and collaborators.

## 5. Inclusion and exclusion rules

A public frontier paper should normally satisfy all of the following:

1. integrability is central to the paper rather than incidental;
2. the work has a clear new contribution;
3. the contribution can be explained accurately from primary sources;
4. the connection to the site's core audience can be stated;
5. the paper provides value beyond a direct arXiv listing.

Strong candidates normally combine:

- **structural innovation**, such as a new spectral problem, hierarchy, Hamiltonian structure, Riemann--Hilbert problem, tau structure, spectral curve, or Yang--Baxter structure;
- **a new consequence**, such as a theorem, asymptotic law, universality result, classification, experiment, inverse method, control mechanism, or macroscopic dynamics.

Papers should normally be excluded when they are mainly:

- a routine application of a standard transformation;
- another small collection of explicit low-order solutions;
- parameter plots without a new structural conclusion;
- a familiar method transferred with little conceptual change;
- only weakly connected to integrability;
- too opaque to explain reliably;
- selected primarily because the equation or author matches the editor's personal interests.

## 6. Minimal site organization

A large redesign is not currently justified. The existing main pages should be retained, with narrower responsibilities.

### 6.1 Homepage: latest frontier

The homepage should become the actual public radar.

It should:

- show only recent qualifying research events;
- sort by actual release, revision, or publication date;
- allow days with no entries;
- display the paper's main result directly;
- display the structural connection and type of innovation directly;
- show whether the annotation is automated or human-reviewed;
- avoid requiring readers to click a detailed page to understand the paper's value.

It should not display old background papers merely because they were recently added to the site.

### 6.2 Research Radar archive: discovery history

The weekly archive should record the public frontier stream over time. It should not be described as a fully reviewed reading guide.

The archive may preserve automatic summaries, but it must state that unmarked entries are machine-assisted and not individually endorsed by the editor.

After the retrospective rewrite, public weekly pages should primarily contain papers that were genuinely new in that period, ordered by actual research date rather than recommendation date.

### 6.3 Core topics: small human-reviewed topics

`Core topics / 核心主题` should remain intentionally small. It should not attempt to map the entire field.

Detailed topic pages should be created only when the editor is prepared to review them. Possible initial topics include:

- gauge relations among the standard DNLS equations;
- spectral and asymptotic structures of the Fokas--Lenells equation;
- coupled and multicomponent NLS systems;
- finite-gap degeneration and soliton gases;
- Riemann--Hilbert steepest descent.

Each topic should contain only human-checked explanations and a short list of human-checked key references.

### 6.4 Group work, resources, and about

These pages can remain largely unchanged. `About` should eventually document:

- frontier selection criteria;
- metadata policy;
- review-status labels;
- the distinction between automatic summaries and human-reviewed material;
- the history of the retrospective archive rewrite.

## 7. Treatment of older papers already in the repository

Removing a paper from the homepage, removing it from a public archive, and deleting it from `data/papers.yml` are different actions.

### 7.1 Human-reviewed and genuinely important older papers

- remove from the latest-frontier homepage;
- retain reliable bibliographic metadata;
- retain Git history and, where appropriate, historical recommendation records;
- add to a specific Core topic only after human review.

### 7.2 Useful but unreviewed background papers

- remove from the latest-frontier homepage;
- do not automatically move into Core topics;
- retain reliable metadata for deduplication and possible future review;
- retain only where the public archive still has a clear historical or reference purpose.

### 7.3 Weak, repetitive, quota-filling, or loosely related recommendations

- remove the public recommendation entry;
- keep Git history as the audit trail;
- retain the bibliographic record only when it is accurate and useful for deduplication.

### 7.4 Duplicate, incorrect, or unverifiable records

- remove from the registry after confirming no remaining references;
- preserve the correction through normal Git history.

Age alone is not a reason to delete a bibliographic record. However, an old paper should not appear in the current frontier stream merely because it was recently discovered.

## 8. Retrospective rewrite of the existing archive

The first retrospective project should cover:

- `2026-W28`: 2026-07-06 through 2026-07-12;
- `2026-W29`: 2026-07-13 through 2026-07-19.

The procedure should be:

1. search primary sources again using the broadened topic scope;
2. record the full screening funnel:
   - raw records inspected;
   - records surviving title screening;
   - records surviving abstract screening;
   - final papers proposed for publication;
3. deduplicate against `data/papers.yml` by arXiv ID, DOI, and normalized title;
4. verify authors, title, version, dates, DOI/journal status, and abstract;
5. divide final selections into core structural frontier and explainable adjacent frontier;
6. remove old background fillers from the public weekly lists;
7. sort entries by actual research-event date;
8. clearly state that these weeks were retrospectively re-edited under a broader policy;
9. preserve prior states in Git history rather than pretending the new selection was the original selection.

The archive should not be rewritten until the screening report has been shown to and accepted by the editor.

## 9. Minimal data and rendering changes under consideration

A full new data model is not yet required. The existing edition entries may initially gain a small number of explicit fields:

```yaml
- paper_id: "arxiv:xxxx.xxxxx"
  signal_date: "2026-07-19"
  signal_type: "new-preprint"
  review_status: "automated"
  frontier_class: "core"
```

Possible values:

- `signal_type`: `new-preprint`, `major-revision`, `journal-publication`;
- `review_status`: `automated`, `human-reviewed`, possibly `pending-review`;
- `frontier_class`: `core`, `adjacent`.

The renderer should eventually:

- select homepage entries by eligibility and actual `signal_date`;
- stop using recommendation date and `homepage_rank` as the main public ordering rule;
- display main result, integrable structure, and innovation type;
- display review status;
- exclude background roles from the homepage.

Whether a separate `data/frontier.yml` is preferable should be decided only after the retrospective trial shows whether the existing edition format remains manageable.

## 10. Automation policy

The daily workflow should become a screening workflow rather than a quota-driven reading-chain generator.

It should:

1. search broadly across the agreed structural topics;
2. create an internal candidate pool;
3. reject routine, weak, or opaque papers;
4. report the screening funnel;
5. publish only strong recent papers;
6. permit an empty day;
7. avoid adding old background papers to complete a theme;
8. keep metadata summaries separate from human editorial judgment;
9. present a reviewable PR and never merge automatically during the current publication phase.

Automatically generated reading chains and background explanations should not be published as human-approved material. They may be retained as drafts or internal aids until reviewed.

## 12. Questions still requiring explicit decisions

These questions should be settled before implementation proceeds beyond the screening report.

### 12.1 Target reader

Is the primary reader best described as:

- a researcher already familiar with integrable PDEs and spectral methods;
- a broader mathematical-physics researcher;
- or a graduate student entering the field?

This determines how much explanation each adjacent-frontier entry needs.

### 12.2 Freshness window

Should the homepage normally cover:

- the most recent 14 days;
- the most recent 21 days;
- the most recent 30 days;
- or a fixed number of qualifying papers independent of date?

### 12.3 Definition of a major revision

What level of arXiv change qualifies as a new frontier signal?

Possible threshold: a new theorem, method, section, experiment, corrected main conclusion, or materially expanded result. Metadata edits and ordinary polishing should not qualify.

### 12.4 Formal journal versions

Should a newly published journal version appear when the underlying preprint is old?

Possible policy: include it only when formal publication adds substantial content, resolves an important uncertainty, or is itself a notable publication event. Otherwise treat it as a metadata refresh.

### 12.5 Core versus adjacent balance

Should the public stream target an approximate balance, such as:

- 70--80% core structural frontier;
- 20--30% explainable adjacent frontier;

or should there be no numerical target and only a quality threshold?

### 12.6 Review-status wording

Which public labels are clearest and least distracting?

Candidates:

- `自动整理` / `人工审校`;
- `AI-assisted summary` / `editor reviewed`;
- a short site-wide notice plus labels only on reviewed entries.

### 12.7 Fate of the original W28 and W29 editorial chains

After the public archive is rewritten, should the original chains:

- exist only in Git history;
- be retained in a separate clearly labeled legacy page;
- or be preserved as unpublished internal notes?

### 12.8 Initial Core topics

Which one or two small topics should be reviewed first? The decision should reflect material the editor already understands well enough to maintain.

### 12.9 Screening transparency

Should each weekly archive publish only the final selected papers, or also a short methodology note with candidate counts and common exclusion reasons?

### 12.10 Separate frontier data file

After the trial rewrite, should frontier signals remain in `data/editions.yml`, or should they move to a separate `data/frontier.yml` so that public frontier events and editorial reading history cannot be mixed again?

## 13. Immediate next step

The next action should not be a site redesign. It should be a **retrospective screening report for W28 and W29**, with no public-content changes.

That report should establish whether the broadened scope produces a selective, understandable, and structurally innovative set of recent papers. Only after reviewing that concrete set should the data model, homepage renderer, and archive be changed.
