# How to use this guide

This guide is designed for research training, not casual browsing. The best way to use it is to identify the mathematical task you are trying to perform and then follow a short, curated path.

## Typical use cases

A reader might use the guide to:

- find lecture notes before reading a paper on inverse scattering or Riemann-Hilbert methods;
- compare PDE-based and IST-based treatments of NLS or DNLS;
- locate course pages with problem sets, slides, or reading lists;
- find workshop pages with talks on nonlinear dispersive PDE or inverse scattering;
- identify background notes on spectral theory, Fourier analysis, complex analysis, or finite-gap methods;
- maintain a personal reading plan for long-time asymptotic analysis.

## Resource levels

Each curated resource should eventually be assigned one of the following levels.

- `introductory`: useful for first contact with the subject.
- `graduate-course`: suitable for systematic study with standard graduate prerequisites.
- `paper-preparation`: useful immediately before reading research papers.
- `research-reference`: useful as a technical reference during research.
- `specialized`: valuable but focused on a narrow method, equation, or literature branch.

## Quality grades

The intended grade scale is:

- `S`: core training resource; maintain carefully and recommend prominently.
- `A`: high-quality resource for a major topic or method.
- `B`: useful local resource for a chapter, lecture, method, or example.
- `C`: background or auxiliary resource.
- `D`: candidate resource that still needs review.
- `reject`: reviewed and not suitable for inclusion.

## Contribution rule

Do not add bare links unless they are explicitly marked as unreviewed candidates. A mature entry should explain why the resource is useful and where it fits mathematically.
